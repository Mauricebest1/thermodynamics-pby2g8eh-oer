Course Introduction: Graduate Chemical Engineering Thermodynamics

Chemical engineering thermodynamics provides the molecular and macroscopic framework for understanding equilibrium, phase behavior, and the driving forces that govern chemical and physical processes. At the graduate level, this course moves beyond the classical application of thermodynamic laws and focuses on the deeper theoretical structure that connects molecular interactions, statistical mechanics, solution behavior, and modern chemical engineering problems.

The course will begin with a rigorous review of thermodynamic potentials, stability criteria, phase equilibrium, and chemical potential. We will then develop the statistical mechanical foundations of thermodynamics, emphasizing how macroscopic properties emerge from molecular configurations and interactions. Building on this foundation, we will examine equations of state, nonideal mixtures, activity and fugacity models, phase transitions, interfacial phenomena, and polymer and electrolyte thermodynamics where appropriate.

A central theme of the course is the connection between molecular structure and thermodynamic behavior. Students will learn how thermodynamic concepts are used to analyze and predict vapor–liquid equilibrium, liquid–liquid separation, adsorption, self-assembly, solvation, and reaction equilibrium. The course will also highlight applications in energy, separations, soft materials, environmental systems, and biochemical engineering.

By the end of the course, students should be able to formulate thermodynamic problems rigorously, select appropriate models for real systems, interpret thermodynamic data critically, and connect molecular-level insight to engineering-scale behavior. The course is intended to prepare graduate students for advanced research in chemical engineering and related fields where thermodynamics remains a fundamental language for describing complex systems.
